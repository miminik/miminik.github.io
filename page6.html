Energy Crisis and the Future Earth

Gravity Battery: A Sustainable Storage Solution

Introduction

As we embrace solar and wind energy, one major challenge remains—how do we store this energy for use when the sun isn’t shining or the wind isn’t blowing? Gravity batteries offer a novel, mechanical solution to this problem. This exhibit presents a working prototype of a gravity battery installed inside a traditional well, using surplus solar or wind energy to lift a heavy weight during the day, and letting gravity lower it at night to generate electricity. It’s a simple, sustainable, and long-lasting alternative to chemical batteries for household-level energy needs.

Exhibit Concept

The exhibit will feature a scaled working model of a gravity battery system installed within a vertical shaft, simulating an old well. A motor powered by a solar panel lifts a weight during the day. At night, the descending weight turns a generator to light up bulbs and power small fans—mimicking household use. A display will show energy input/output, battery charge levels, and comparison with lithium-ion systems.

Interactive Element

Visitors can operate a crank or switch to activate the lifting system during the day phase and then manually release the weight to see nighttime generation. An interactive touchscreen lets users configure parameters like well depth, weight mass, and energy demand for a family of four. A simulation calculates how long power would last, what appliances can be run, and carbon emissions saved compared to a diesel generator or lithium-ion battery.

Scientific Comparison

Parameter Lithium-Ion Battery Gravity Battery
Storage Duration 5–10 years 20+ years with minimal maintenance
Efficiency 85–95% 70–85%
Environmental Impact High (mining, disposal) Low (uses concrete/metal weights)
Cost over Lifetime ₹5–7 per kWh stored ₹2–3 per kWh stored

According to Wikipedia and a 2021 report by the World Economic Forum, gravity-based systems can outperform lithium batteries in terms of long-term sustainability, maintenance, and recyclability—especially in remote or off-grid applications.

Design Details for a Family

For a four-member household consuming ~3.5 kWh/day, a 25-meter-deep well with a 500 kg weight can deliver up to 1.5 kWh of usable energy per descent. Coupled with rooftop solar and wind micro-turbines, the system can recharge daily and cover basic needs like LED lighting, phone charging, fan operation, and television. The battery can be scaled modularly based on depth, weight, and motor size.
